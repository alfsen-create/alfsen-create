Project Atlas Trials: Patient-Centric MVP Data Collection Platform for Off-Label Drug Research (Revised Proposal)
Date: May 27, 2025

Overall Goal: To establish a secure, user-friendly, and robust data collection platform that enables patients and caregivers to systematically log drug usage, side effects, and health outcomes for off-label treatments. This platform aims to gather actionable insights, foster "citizen science," bridge the gap between anecdotal evidence and structured research, and contribute to the body of knowledge on repurposed drugs for conditions like late-stage cancer and Alzheimer's disease.

Part 1: MVP Data Collection Platform Proposal
1. Scientific Context: Repurposed Drugs for Critical Illnesses
A. Antiparasitics for Cancer: Ivermectin & Fenbendazole
Ivermectin: Some in vitro and small animal studies suggest potential anti-cancer effects (e.g., modulating cell signaling, inducing apoptosis). However, there is a lack of large, high-quality clinical trials demonstrating meaningful survival benefits in humans with cancer.
Fenbendazole: An animal dewormer. Case reports and preclinical studies have shown tumor inhibition. Robust human trial data is absent, though anecdotal evidence is prevalent online. Peer-reviewed evidence remains limited and primarily preclinical.
Note on selection: These examples, along with those below, are illustrative of drugs with significant patient interest and some preclinical data, highlighting the need for more structured real-world evidence.
B. Metformin & GLP-1 Agonists for Alzheimer’s Disease
Metformin: Observational studies in diabetic populations suggest a possible protective effect against dementia. A few pilot Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) exist for Alzheimer’s. Meta-analyses show some cognitive benefits, but results are mixed, and optimal dosage/timing remains unclear.
GLP-1 Agonists (e.g., Semaglutide): A growing body of preclinical and early clinical data suggests these may reduce neuroinflammation and slow cognitive decline. Major trials are currently underway (e.g., EVOKE and EVOKE+ trials for semaglutide in early Alzheimer's).
Summary of Evidence
Many repurposed drugs exhibit theoretical or preliminary evidence for off-label applications in severe diseases. However, large-scale, definitive RCTs are often scarce. This gap is frequently due to a lack of commercial incentives, regulatory complexities, and the inherent difficulties in studying critically ill populations.

2. Challenges in Off-Label and Compassionate Use Trials
Regulatory Hurdles: While FDA-approved drugs can be legally prescribed off-label, systematically studying them and using the data for new claims is tightly regulated.
Data Quality Concerns: Anecdotal reports and "n-of-1" cases rarely provide high-quality, actionable evidence. Standardized protocols are crucial to mitigate self-report bias, placebo effects, and confounding variables.
Ethical Considerations: In situations involving terminally ill patients, the dynamics of informed consent and risk/benefit assessments shift. Clinicians, institutions, and Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) often proceed with caution to avoid legal issues or negative publicity.
Logistical Complexities: Coordinating large-scale participation, ensuring consistent data collection, and maintaining adherence are significant operational challenges, especially in decentralized, patient-driven initiatives.

3. Building a Protocol for Real-World Data Collection
In an era of AI, digital health, and self-tracking, decentralized, patient-driven trials are increasingly feasible, particularly for populations with limited therapeutic options.
A. Core Protocol Components
Eligibility Criteria:
Confirmed diagnosis of late-stage cancer or Alzheimer’s disease (or other targeted conditions).
Willingness to try an off-label FDA-approved drug following comprehensive informed consent.
Drug Administration:
Clearly specified (ideally standardized) dosage, timing, and co-medications.
Source of Medication: Participants will typically be using medication prescribed by their physician and obtained through regular channels. The platform does not supply or endorse specific sources of medication.
Outcome Tracking:
Regular monitoring of survival, functional status, cognition, and side effects (utilizing validated scales where possible).
Frequent digital check-ins via self-report or caregiver input.
Data Capture:
Use of a secure, standardized application or platform (mobile, web, or voice-activated).
AI-assisted prompts, reminders, and data collection.
Open database structure with robust privacy safeguards.
Oversight and Ethics:
Central IRB approval, even for decentralized "virtual" studies, is ideal.
Ongoing monitoring for potential harm, with clear mechanisms for patient withdrawal.
Data Governance: A clear data governance plan will outline data access, usage, and sharing policies, overseen by the ethics advisory board or IRB.
B. Example Protocol: Metformin in Alzheimer’s Disease
Inclusion Criteria: Diagnosed Alzheimer’s, age >60, Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) score <24, not currently on metformin.
Intervention: Metformin XR, titrated up to 1000mg twice daily (BID).
Duration: 12 months.
Outcome Measures: Changes in MMSE, Activities of Daily Living (ADL) scores, caregiver reports, and documented side effects.
Data Entry: Weekly via app/caregiver; quarterly telehealth or in-person check-ins.

4. Platform Implementation: Practical and Legal Strategies
Patient-Led Initiatives: Models like PeopleWith, OpenHumans, and CureTogether demonstrate successful decentralized data collection. The Nightscout Project (diabetes technology) is a classic example of a patient-led trial.
IRB/Ethics Approval:
Seek partnerships with digital health nonprofits, universities, or “virtual” IRBs for ethical and legal oversight.
Ensure patients are fully informed that the study is exploratory and data may not yield immediate clinical benefits.
Open-Source Platform Development:
Utilize or build a digital platform (e.g., REDCap, custom web app, or initially even Google Forms) for data entry and tracking. This approach can enhance transparency, foster community trust and contribution, and allow for greater adaptability.
Strategic Partnerships:
Collaborate with advocacy groups, universities, or health tech startups to enhance legitimacy and access larger patient pools.
Publication and Data Sharing:
Plan for open-access publication of results, irrespective of the study's statistical power.
Pre-register protocols on platforms like clinicaltrials.gov or ISRCTN.

5. Key Risks and Mitigation Strategies
Legal/Regulatory:
Risk: Making unapproved treatment claims.
Mitigation: Clearly state that the platform facilitates data gathering and "citizen science," not medical advice or treatment endorsements.
Data Quality:
Risk: Inaccuracies from self-report bias or inconsistent entries.
Mitigation: Employ digital tools and AI to prompt/validate entries. Use standardized scales and clear instructions. While primarily relying on patient-reported data for the MVP, future iterations may explore opt-in, verifiable data points (e.g., clinician confirmation of diagnosis, with explicit consent), balanced with privacy considerations and MVP scope.
Safety:
Risk: Adverse events or harm to participants; misinterpretation of data by users.
Mitigation: Provide clear guidance for when to stop the intervention or seek medical attention. Have a medical doctor or advisory board available for triage and consultation for platform-related safety queries only, not for individual medical advice. The platform will prominently display disclaimers that it does not offer medical advice and that all treatment decisions and urgent concerns must be discussed with the participant's physician or emergency services. Risk of misinterpretation of personal data by users will be mitigated through clear presentation and educational resources.
Equity:
Risk: System accessibility issues for diverse patient and caregiver backgrounds.
Mitigation: Design the platform for ease of use, considering various levels of tech literacy, language, and physical abilities. Offer support and alternative input methods if feasible. Consider offering multilingual support for key interface elements in future iterations, based on user demographics.

8. MVP Data Collection Platform: Detailed Overview
Goal: To enable patients and caregivers to easily, securely, and consistently log drug use, side effects, and outcomes, facilitating the generation and sharing of actionable insights.
A. Core Features
User Types:
Patients / Caregivers: Input data, view personal dashboard, manage consent.
Researchers / Admins: Monitor anonymized aggregate data, export reports, manage participant communications (without accessing identifiable data unless explicitly permitted for safety monitoring by an ethics board). Adherence to strict data privacy and de-identification protocols is mandatory for this role.
Key Functionalities:
Secure Registration & Consent: Email-based registration, optional two-factor authentication (2FA), e-signature for digital consent forms. Consent forms will be version-controlled, and re-consent will be sought for material changes to protocols or data usage.
Protocol Assignment: Users can choose or be assigned to a specific study protocol (e.g., “Metformin for Alzheimer’s”).
Data Entry Forms: Simple, mobile-friendly forms for:
Medication log (drug, dose, time taken).
Symptom/outcome tracking (using validated scales, numeric ratings, and open comments).
Side effects/adverse event reporting.
Optional: Secure upload for lab results, imaging reports, or doctor’s notes (with explicit consent per upload).
Automated Reminders: SMS, push notifications, or email nudges to prompt data entry to help ensure consistent data entry and reduce participant burden.
Personal Dashboard: Visual progress tracking for individuals (e.g., “Days on protocol,” “Last cognitive test score,” symptom trends).
Aggregate Dashboard (Researchers/Public): De-identified, aggregate statistics (e.g., percentage reporting improvement, frequency of side effects).
Data Export: CSV download of anonymized aggregate data for further analysis (admins/researchers only); individuals can download their own data.
B. User Journey
Sign Up / Log In: Secure account creation.
Informed Consent: Read and digitally sign the consent form.
Profile Setup: Basic demographic and diagnostic information, baseline functional/cognitive status, current medications (optional contact for study team).
Protocol Selection: Choose or confirm the drug protocol they are joining. View overview: purpose, expected procedures, potential risks, and contact for questions.
Baseline Data Entry: Log baseline symptoms, functional/cognitive status.
Regular Check-Ins (e.g., Daily/Weekly):
Complete a simple, repeatable form: "Did you take your medication? Dose? Any side effects? How do you feel today? Changes in function?"
Complete any scheduled cognitive or symptom tests.
Option for caregiver to log data on behalf of the patient.
Alerts & Safety Monitoring:
Emergency contact information and a feature to "flag" concerning symptoms for review.
Automated alert to study admin/safety monitor if high-risk data is entered (pre-defined triggers).
View Results: Users can see their own trends over time. Aggregated, de-identified data is shared openly on the site or through reports.
C. Proposed MVP Tech Stack
Frontend: React (web, mobile-optimized).
Consider: MUI or Chakra UI for UI components; React Router for navigation.
Forms: React Hook Form for robust and accessible forms.
Backend: Node.js (Express) or Firebase Cloud Functions.
Rationale: Firebase Cloud Functions can handle most business logic for an MVP, reducing server maintenance.
Database: Firebase Firestore.
Rationale: NoSQL, scalable, real-time updates, strong security integration with Firebase Auth.
Key: Implement Firestore security rules early to ensure data protection.
Authentication: Firebase Authentication (supports email/password, Google, Facebook, Apple, magic links, 2FA).
Data Visualization: Google Charts, Recharts, or Chart.js for frontend dashboards.
Hosting: Firebase Hosting.
Rationale: Fast, secure, global CDN, easy deployment for frontend and backend functions, generous free tier.
Security:
HTTPS by default with Firebase Hosting.
Data encryption at rest and in transit (provided by Firebase).
Strong authentication mechanisms.
Regular security audits (even if self-audits for MVP) and adherence to best practices for secure software development.
Error Tracking: Sentry or Firebase Crashlytics (for mobile aspects if extended, Sentry is excellent for web).
Consent Management: Store digital consent (e.g., PDF or structured data) in Firebase Storage or Firestore, linking it to the user's profile.

Firestore Security Rules (Conceptual):

Protocols: Readable by all authenticated users, writable only by admins.

F. Privacy & Ethics Considerations
All data encrypted at rest and in transit.
Users can download or request deletion of their own data.
No personally identifiable information (PII) is published in aggregate reports.
Research data is de-identified and aggregated.
Explicit digital informed consent is mandatory before participation.
Clear guidance for participants to consult their physician before joining any protocol and for any medical concerns. The platform explicitly does not provide medical advice. Participants are consistently reminded to consult their healthcare providers for any medical decisions or concerns.

Participate: Select a protocol (e.g., based on a condition like 'Early Alzheimer's' and a drug they are already using like 'Metformin').
Report: Log medication details, symptoms, and outcomes with automated reminders.
Learn & Contribute: Track personal progress visually and contribute to a larger pool of anonymized data for community insights.
Who Should Get Involved?
Patients & Caregivers: Facing late-stage cancer, Alzheimer’s, or other conditions with high unmet needs, and exploring or using off-label treatments under physician guidance.
Patient Advocacy Groups: Seeking to empower their communities and advance research.
Clinicians & Researchers: Interested in real-world evidence and drug repurposing.
Tech Partners, Nonprofits & Visionary Funders: Eager to support innovative, patient-centered research.
